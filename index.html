Meaning, functions, scope, decisions, performance objectives & productivity

Lecture 1
What is Operations Management?
Core Definition

"Operations management is the activity of managing the resources which are devoted to the production and delivery of products and services." — Slack et al.

Alternative Definition

"The business function responsible for planning, coordinating, and controlling the resources needed to produce a company's products and services." — Wiley, 2005

Why is Operations Management Important?

OM is not just a back-office function — it directly impacts a company's financial health and competitive position in four key ways:

1. Reduces Costs

By producing products and services efficiently, OM lowers the cost base. A manufacturer that wastes less material, uses energy efficiently, and keeps machinery running makes more profit per unit sold.

3. Reduces Investment Needs

By increasing the effective capacity of existing operations and finding innovative uses of physical resources, OM reduces how much new investment is needed to grow output.

2. Increases Revenue

High quality and excellent service win and retain customers. Every complaint resolved, every order delivered on time, and every product without defects translates directly into repeat business and positive referrals.

4. Enhances Innovation

A strong operational base — skilled staff, efficient processes, deep technical knowledge — creates the foundation from which new products, services, and ways of working can emerge.

Example — IKEA's Operations

IKEA demonstrates all four simultaneously: flat-pack design reduces shipping costs (cost), attractive products and good service drive footfall (revenue), large stores with efficient replenishment maximise throughput from existing assets (investment), and continuous review of operations keeps IKEA at the frontier of retail innovation (innovation).

The 4 Vs Framework — Profiling Operations

Volume, Variety, Variation and Visibility

The four Vs help describe any operation and reveal its strategic implications. Understanding your own 4 Vs profile — and whether it is where you want to be — is a key management skill.

📊 Volume

How many units are produced? High volume = high repeatability = specialisation possible = lower unit cost via economies of scale.

Low Volume
High Volume
Low

A bespoke jeweller crafts 3 rings per week.

High

Nestlé produces millions of KitKat bars per day — same process, same equipment, every time.

🎨 Variety

How many different products or services are offered? High variety means each customer/product may need a different process, raising cost but improving satisfaction for complex needs.

Low Variety

A budget airline — one class, no meals, no reserved seats. Simple, cheap.

High Variety

A private bank tailors financial packages to each client's unique wealth profile.

📈 Variation in Demand

How much does demand fluctuate? High variation requires flexible capacity — operations must be able to scale up and down, which is costly and complex to manage.

Low Variation

An electricity grid — demand is fairly predictable, allowing very high resource utilisation and low unit cost.

High Variation

A tax accounting firm: swamped in January–April, near-empty in August. Must hire temps, lose efficiency.

👁️ Visibility (Customer Contact)

How much of the operation does the customer see and experience directly? High visibility demands customer-contact skills and quick response; it cannot be "industrialised" easily.

Low Visibility

An Amazon fulfilment centre — customers never see picking, packing, or logistics operations.

High Visibility

A GP surgery — patient is present throughout; every interaction must be patient-centred and empathetic.

Strategic Implication

Moving towards the high-volume / low-variety end gives lower unit costs but less flexibility. Moving towards low-volume / high-variety gives responsiveness but higher costs. Operations managers must consciously choose — and manage — their position on each V.

Formule 1 Hotel vs Agua Safari Hotel

Formule 1 (budget chain): High volume, Low variety, Low variation, Low visibility → standardised rooms, automated check-in, predictable demand, low staff contact → very low unit cost per room night.

Agua Safari (luxury resort): Low volume, High variety, High variation (seasonal peaks), High visibility → bespoke experiences, personal butlers, unpredictable bookings → very high unit cost, but premium pricing justifies it.

Operations Strategy

What is Strategy in Operations?

Definition

"Strategy consists of competitive efforts and business approaches that managers employ to please customers, compete successfully and achieve organisational objectives." — Thompson & Strickland, 2003

Operations management has three strategic roles:

1. Implementing Strategy

Operations carries out what the corporate strategy decides. If the strategy is to compete on low cost, operations must deliver efficiency.

2. Supporting Strategy

Operations develops capabilities that support the strategic position, e.g., building quality systems that support a premium brand strategy.

3. Driving Strategy

Operations capabilities become so distinctive they drive strategy — e.g., Toyota's lean manufacturing became the basis of its global competitive advantage.

Types of Operations Strategy

  • New product development strategy — When and how to bring new products/services to market
  • Vertical integration strategy — Whether to make components in-house or buy from suppliers
  • Facilities strategy — Number, size, type, and location of facilities
  • Technological strategy — Which process technologies to adopt
  • Capacity adjustment strategy — How quickly and by how much to grow or shrink capacity
  • Inventory strategy — What to stock, how much, and where
  • Improvement strategy — Which improvement methods to use (lean, Six Sigma, etc.)

Strategic vs. Tactical OM Decisions

Operations decisions exist at two levels. Strategic decisions create the framework; tactical decisions operate within it day to day.

STRATEGIC DECISIONS

Broad in scope · Long-term · All encompassing

  • Product/Service design
  • Capacity planning (how much can we make?)
  • Facility location (where should we operate?)
  • Facility layout (how should we arrange space?)
  • Job design (how should work be structured?)
Example

"What unique features of our product make us competitive in the next 5 years?"

TACTICAL DECISIONS

Narrow in scope · Short-term · Specific issues

  • Quality control (are we meeting standards?)
  • Demand forecasting (what will customers want?)
  • Supply chain management (who supplies us?)
  • Production planning (what do we make this week?)
  • Inventory control (how much to stock?)
  • Scheduling (who does what and when?)
Example

"Who will work the second shift tomorrow?"

The Five Performance Objectives

How Operations Competes

All operations compete — whether externally against rivals or internally as a support function — on five fundamental dimensions. These are not equally important to all customers or all markets; the skill lies in understanding which matter most and excelling at those.

🎯

1. Quality — "Doing things right"

Producing products and services that conform to specification, are free from defects, and meet or exceed customer expectations. Quality affects both costs (fewer errors = less rework) and revenue (satisfied customers return).
Steel Plant: % of products conforming to exact specification
Mortgage Service: Accuracy of information; professionalism of staff
Finance Function: Accuracy of reports; insightfulness of advice
Restaurant: Consistently good food; no food safety incidents
⚡

2. Speed — "Doing things fast"

Minimising the time between a customer requesting something and receiving it. Speed reduces in-process inventory, cuts throughput time, and directly impacts customer satisfaction (nobody likes waiting).
Steel Plant: Lead time from enquiry to quotation; order to delivery
Mortgage Service: Time for call centre to respond; fast loan decisions
Finance Function: Responsiveness to other departments' queries
Hospital A&E: Time from arrival to first seen by clinician
🔒

3. Dependability — "Doing things on time"

Keeping promises. Delivering what was promised, when it was promised, every time. Underpins customer trust — a dependable supplier becomes embedded in customers' own planning. Internally, dependability saves time and money by avoiding disruption and rescheduling.
Steel Plant: % of deliveries "on time, in full"; customers kept informed
Mortgage Service: Reliability of promised completion date
Finance Function: Financial information available exactly when needed for meetings
Delivery Company: Next-day guarantee; tracking accuracy
🔄

4. Flexibility — "Changing what you do"

The ability to change operations — what you produce, how much, when, or at what speed — in response to changing circumstances. There are four types: product/service flexibility, mix flexibility, volume flexibility, and delivery flexibility.
Steel Plant: Range of sizes, gauges, coatings possible; ability to change timing
Mortgage Service: Customise loan terms; cope with changes in customer circumstances
Finance Function: Ability to adjust volume of work to meet tight deadlines
Netflix: Can scale streaming capacity instantly during unexpected demand spikes
💰

5. Cost — "Doing things cheaply"

Minimising the cost of producing each unit of output. For companies competing on price, low cost is the primary objective. Even for premium providers, lower costs mean higher margins. Improving the other four objectives also tends to reduce cost (fewer defects = less scrap; more speed = less inventory).
Steel Plant: Price of products; discounts available; payment terms
Mortgage Service: Interest rate charged; arrangement fees
Finance Function: Cost per transaction; headcount & facilities cost
Ryanair: Strips all non-essential costs to offer market-lowest fares

Factors That Influence Which Objectives Matter Most

Customer Influence

Order-winning factors directly win orders — customers choose you because of them (e.g. lowest price, fastest delivery). Qualifying factors are minimum standards — you must meet them to be considered, but excelling doesn't win extra business (e.g. basic quality compliance).

Competitor Behaviour

If a competitor dramatically improves their speed or cuts prices, you must respond or lose market share. Operations must continuously monitor competitive moves and adjust its own performance priorities accordingly.

Product Life Cycle Stage

Introduction: Flexibility & quality matter most. Growth: Speed and quality. Maturity: Dependability and cost. Decline: Cost becomes dominant as price wars intensify.

The Triple Bottom Line

Beyond Profit: Operations and Sustainability

Modern operations management is accountable not just to shareholders but to society and the planet. The Triple Bottom Line framework measures success across three dimensions:

💰

Economic Bottom Line

Traditional profit maximisation. Generating returns for shareholders and ensuring the business is financially sustainable.

Measured by: profit margin, ROI, earnings per share, revenue growth

🤝

Social Bottom Line

How the business impacts communities, employees, and society. Balancing profit with ethical responsibility — fair wages, safe working conditions, community investment.

Measured by: employee satisfaction, community investment, fair trade compliance, labour standards in supply chain

🌿

Environmental Bottom Line

Minimising the ecological impact of operations — carbon emissions, water usage, waste, toxic materials, end-of-life product management.

Measured by: carbon footprint, water usage, waste sent to landfill, energy consumption per unit, recyclability of products

Example — Unilever

Unilever's Sustainable Living Plan commits to: halving environmental footprint (environmental), improving wellbeing of 1 billion people (social), while doubling the business (economic). Their operations must deliver all three simultaneously.

Linking Vision & Strategy to Operations Performance

The Balanced Scorecard (Kaplan & Norton) translates an organisation's vision and strategy into a comprehensive set of performance measures across four perspectives. Operations contributes to — and is measured in — all four.

E.g. reduce unit cost by 10%, increase ROI

👥 Customer

Why the BSC matters to Operations Managers

The BSC prevents "financial tunnel vision." An operation that only tracks profit might cut training (harming learning & growth), delay maintenance (harming internal processes), and miss delivery targets (harming customers) — all to hit short-term financial numbers that lead to long-term failure. The BSC forces a holistic view.

Productivity in Operations

Productivity is the fundamental measure of operational efficiency:

Productivity (P) = Outputs ÷ Inputs

A higher ratio means getting more out of the same (or less) input — the core goal of operations improvement

Types of Productivity Measurement

Single-Factor Productivity

Measures output against one specific input.

Labour Productivity

Factory makes 600 units with 10 workers = 60 units per worker. After a new layout redesign: 900 units with same 10 workers = 90 units per worker → 50% productivity gain.

Multi-Factor Productivity

Measures output against multiple combined inputs.

Example

A call centre tracks calls handled per GH₵ of total cost (wages + technology + rent). This captures trade-offs — e.g. investing in better software raises costs but also raises calls handled.

Total Factor Productivity

Measures all outputs against all inputs — the most comprehensive view.

Example

An airline tracks total revenue passengers flown ÷ total operating costs. Improvements in fuel efficiency, routing, or aircraft utilisation all show up here.

Ways to Improve Productivity
  • Lean techniques: Eliminate waste (overproduction, waiting, unnecessary transport, defects, over-processing, excess inventory, unused talent)
  • Process redesign: Restructure tasks to reduce steps and rework
  • Technology investment: Automate repetitive tasks to raise output without proportional increase in labour cost
  • Better scheduling: Match staffing to demand so idle time is minimised
  • Staff training: Skilled workers make fewer errors and work faster
  • Capacity management: Run closer to full capacity to spread fixed costs over more units

Introduction to Process Design

All Operations Managers are Designers

Design is not just for product engineers. Operations managers design processes — the sequences, resources, and arrangements that create and deliver products and services.

Process Design Defined

"The process of satisfying people's requirements through the shaping or configuring of products, services, and processes" — including the arrangement of processes, technology, and people that constitute an operation.

Process Design Involves:
  • Envisaging the general shape and intention before defining details
  • Starting with design objectives as the anchor point
  • Analysing volume & variety — the key determinants of process type
  • Ensuring the process fulfils its objectives effectively and efficiently
Heart transplant vs. flu vaccination

Heart transplant: One patient, unique surgery, multiple specialist surgeons, hours of operating, extensive post-op care. Process is completely customised.

Flu vaccination programme: Thousands of patients, same vaccine, same process, nurses trained in one specific task. Process is fully standardised. Same "product" (immunisation), completely different process.

Product/Service Design and Process Design are Interrelated

Products should be designed so they CAN be efficiently produced

If a product designer creates something that is impossible to make at scale, the operation will be slow and costly. "Design for manufacture" principles ensure producibility.

Processes should be designed so they CAN produce all likely future products

A process locked into today's products may be obsolete when the product range changes. Future flexibility must be designed in from the start.

Apple iPhone Example

Apple's product designers want slim, seamless bodies with no visible screws. This directly constrains the assembly process — robots must be precise, components custom-designed, and assembly cells configured for delicate handling.

Conversely, Apple's operations team designed Foxconn's factories knowing that annual iPhone iterations would require rapid reconfiguration — so flexibility was built into the process design, not just the product.

Environmentally Sensitive Design

Both product and process designers must account for environmental ("green") issues throughout the product/service lifecycle:

  • Sources of inputs: Where do raw materials come from? Are they ethically and sustainably sourced?
  • Energy consumption: How much energy does the process use? Can it be reduced or from renewable sources?
  • Waste generation: What waste materials are created during manufacturing? Can they be reduced or recycled?
  • Product life: How long does the product last? Longer life = less replacement = less waste.
  • End-of-life: Can the product be disassembled and recycled? Are toxic materials used?
  • Toxic materials: Protecting employees and the public from immediate and long-term harm.
Example — Patagonia

Patagonia designs products to last decades (product life), uses recycled polyester from plastic bottles (sourcing), runs a "Worn Wear" repair programme (end-of-life), and uses water-based dyes in processes (waste/toxic). All are process design decisions with environmental intent.

Specific Process Design Objectives

Process designers must consider four key operational measures when configuring a process:

Throughput Rate (Flow Rate)

The rate at which units emerge from the process — units per hour, patients per day, etc. This must match customer demand.

A passport office processes 200 applications/day. Demand is 250/day → queue builds. Process redesign needed to raise throughput rate.

Throughput Time

The average elapsed time from when inputs enter the process to when they leave as outputs. Customers want this to be short; operations want it short too (less in-process inventory).

In a fast-food restaurant, throughput time = time from order to food handed over. Target: under 3 minutes. Exceeding this → customer dissatisfaction.

Work-in-Process (WIP) Inventory

The number of units inside the process at any one time (average over a period). High WIP means things are waiting — wasted time, space, and working capital.

A hospital's A&E with 60 patients "in the system" (some waiting for doctors, some for beds, some for results) = high WIP = poor patient experience & high cost.

Resource Utilisation

The proportion of available time that resources (people, machines, space) are performing useful work. Higher utilisation = lower unit cost, but 100% utilisation = no flexibility buffer.

An MRI scanner costing £1M/year running at 50% utilisation costs £2 per minute of actual scanning. At 80% utilisation, cost drops to £1.25 per minute — significant for NHS budgets.

Project

Project Processes

One-off, complex, large-scale outputs with high work content. Defined start and finish — with time, quality, and cost objectives. Many different skills must be coordinated simultaneously. Every "product" is unique and specially made.

Real Examples
  • 🏗️ Building the Accra Metro rail system
  • 🏥 Performing an organ transplant
  • 🎬 Producing a feature film
  • 🚀 Launching a space satellite
  • 🖥️ Implementing an ERP system in a company

Key characteristics: very high unit cost; each is a one-off; no repetition; complex scheduling of diverse specialists

Jobbing

Jobbing Processes

Very small quantities — "one-offs" or just a few. Every output is highly customised. Skill requirements are broad — the skilled worker or team typically completes the whole product. Low volume, high variety.

Real Examples
  • ✂️ Made-to-measure tailor crafting a wedding suit
  • 🔧 Precision engineer building a prototype component
  • 🛠️ Specialist toolmaker fabricating a custom jig
  • 🎨 Artist commissioned for a unique sculpture
  • 🖨️ Small print shop producing a one-off bespoke booklet

Key characteristics: high unit cost; highly skilled generalist workers; low systemisation; no repeatability

Batch

Batch Processes

Higher volumes and lower variety than jobbing. Products are standard with repeating demand, but different batches can be produced. Specialised, narrower skills. Set-ups (changeovers) required between batches. This is the most common manufacturing process type.

Real Examples
  • 🍞 Bakery making batches of sourdough, then switching to croissants
  • 🖨️ Commercial printer running 1,000 brochures, then reconfiguring for posters
  • 📚 University: each course/class is a "batch" of students through a similar process
  • 💊 Pharmaceutical company making batches of paracetamol tablets
  • 👗 Clothing manufacturer producing seasonal collections

Key characteristics: medium unit cost; changeover time between batches is a key efficiency lever (SMED — Single Minute Exchange of Die)

Mass

Mass (Line) Processes

Higher volumes than batch with lower variety. Products are standard, repeat "runners." No significant set-ups — or instantaneous ones. Workers perform narrow, specialised tasks repeatedly. Flow is continuous through the line.

Real Examples
  • 🚗 Toyota assembly line — same car model, millions per year
  • 🍔 McDonald's kitchen — same burgers made the same way at every location
  • 🥤 Coca-Cola bottling plant
  • 📱 Foxconn iPhone assembly lines
  • 📦 Amazon fulfilment centre packing line

Key characteristics: low unit cost; repetitive work; high capital investment; any single failure halts the whole line (bottleneck management critical)

Continuous

Continuous Processes

Extremely high volumes with single or very few products. Products are usually inseparable — they flow continuously rather than as discrete units. Highly capital-intensive and automated. Very few changeovers. Extremely difficult and expensive to start up and shut down — often run 24 hours/day, 365 days/year.

Real Examples
  • 🛢️ Oil refinery — crude oil flows continuously through distillation columns
  • 💧 Municipal water treatment plant
  • 📰 Newsprint paper mill — paper produced as a continuous web
  • ⚡ Nuclear power station — continuous electricity generation
  • 🧪 Chemicals plant — producing bulk commodity chemicals

Key characteristics: lowest unit cost at scale; but highly inflexible to design changes; very high fixed costs; susceptible to component failures (redundancy essential); stopping = enormous cost.

Service Process Types

Services have their own equivalent process types, based on the same volume-variety logic:

Professional Services

Low volume (few clients), high customisation, highly skilled professionals, each engagement unique.

Management consultancy, specialist medical consultants, barristers, architects, independent financial advisors

Service Shops

Moderate volume, some standardisation but with customisable elements within a standard framework.

Health club (standard equipment but each member designs their own workout), mid-range restaurant, bank branch, GP surgery, car dealership service centre

Mass Services

Very high volume, low customisation. Standardisation is key to keeping costs low and throughput high.

Call centres, public bus/rail transport, supermarkets, fast-food chains, budget airlines, post offices

Why this matters strategically

A company that chooses continuous processes gains low unit cost but loses flexibility. A surge in demand for a different product variant cannot be accommodated without expensive retooling. Intermittent operations are the opposite — more flexible but inherently more expensive per unit. There is no "right" answer — it depends on market requirements.

The Product–Process Matrix: Staying "On the Diagonal"

There is a "natural" fit between volume/variety characteristics and process type. Deviating from this natural alignment has serious cost and performance consequences.

Too Much Flexibility for Volume (Above Diagonal)

Using a jobbing process for what should be a batch operation. Example: Using a bespoke tailoring approach to produce 10,000 identical school uniforms — massively over-engineered, impossibly expensive.

Too Little Flexibility for Variety (Below Diagonal)

Using a mass production line for what requires a batch process. Example: Running a rigid assembly line but offering 200 different product variants — constant changeovers cripple efficiency; you have neither low cost nor true flexibility.

Key Insight

Operations must honestly assess their actual volume and variety requirements, then select the process type that fits. Misalignment is one of the most common — and costly — operations management mistakes.

Prêt a Manger Process Maps — Three Approaches Compared
Standard (Pre-Made) Process:

Raw materials → Assembly (batch) → Store sandwiches → Transport to outlet → Store → Customer selects → Sell → Take payment

Efficient for high-volume standard lines; wastes product if demand changes.

Old Customised Process:

Raw materials → Customer requests → Assembly (one at a time) → Take payment

Highly flexible but slow; creates queues during peak periods.

New Customised Process:

Bread + base filling → Pre-assemble bases → Store → Customer arrives → Decision: Standard base? → Yes: Add fillings → Pay. No: Assemble whole sandwich → Add fillings → Pay

Balances speed and customisation — best of both.

Using Process Maps to Improve

Once mapped, look for: delays (waiting that adds no value), unnecessary transport (moving things that shouldn't be moved), redundant inspections (checking what should be correct first time), and decision complexity (branches that slow flow). Each is an improvement opportunity.

Service Blueprinting

Service blueprinting extends process mapping specifically for services, by separating what the customer sees (front stage) from what happens behind the scenes (backstage). This is critical because customers experience and judge only the front-stage.

Six Steps to Develop a Blueprint:
  1. Identify all key activities in creating and delivering the service
  2. Define the "big picture" before drilling down to detail
  3. Distinguish between front stage (customer-visible) and backstage (invisible to customer)
  4. Clarify interactions between customers and staff, and how backstage supports them
  5. Identify potential fail points and plan contingencies
  6. Develop execution standards — maximum wait times, task completion times, staff scripts
Front Stage (visible to customer)

Reservations, greeting at door, valet parking, coat check, table service, food presentation, billing. Customer experiences and judges ALL of these.

Backstage (invisible to customer)

Maintaining reservation systems, taking car to parking lot, kitchen food preparation, dishwashing, HR management, supplier deliveries. Customer never sees these — but they enable the front stage.

Restaurant Blueprint Example

Timeline: Make Reservation → Valet Parking → Coat Room → Seated → Order taken → Food served → Dessert → Bill → Farewell

Line of interaction (customer ↔ staff): All the above steps

Line of visibility: Separates what customer sees from backstage

Line of internal interaction: Separates front-stage from support systems (kitchen, IT, HR)

Fail point example: If the reservation system double-books a table (backstage failure), the customer arrives to find no table — a catastrophic front-stage failure despite front-stage staff doing nothing wrong.

Layout and Flow

What is Layout?

Definition

The layout of an operation means how its transforming resources are positioned relative to each other and how various tasks are allocated to those resources. It dictates the pattern of flow for transformed resources as they move through the operation.

Consequences of Poor Layout
  • Over-long or confused flow patterns
  • Customer queues and long waiting times
  • Long process times and inflexibility
  • Unpredictable flow and high unit cost
  • Difficulty of supervision and management
Why Layout Decisions Are Difficult
  • Equipment may be large, heavy, expensive to move
  • Disruption during relayout can lose customers
  • Complex flow can evolve subtly over years — "layout drift"
  • What works today may not work as the product mix changes
  • Re-laying out an existing operation carries both financial and operational risk

What Makes a Good Layout?

  • Inherent safety: Safe flows with no risk of accidents; clear emergency exits
  • Short flow lengths: Minimise the distance transformed resources travel — shorter = faster, cheaper
  • Clarity of flow: Clear signposting; staff and customers always know where to go next
  • Staff conditions: Pleasant, safe, ergonomic workspace that supports well-being and productivity
  • Management coordination: Layout should facilitate supervision and communication between teams
  • Accessibility: Equipment and areas accessible for proper cleaning, inspection, and maintenance
  • Efficient use of space: Maximise the proportion of space that adds value (vs. wasted corridor space)
  • Long-term flexibility: Ability to reconfigure without enormous cost as products and volumes change
The Four Basic Layout Types
📍

1. Fixed-Position Layout

The transformed resource (product or customer) is stationary — it is too large, delicate, or difficult to move. All transforming resources (workers, tools, equipment, materials) must travel to it.

✅ Advantages
  • Very high product and mix flexibility
  • Product/customer not moved — avoids damage
  • High variety of tasks for staff; skilled, engaged workers
❌ Disadvantages
  • Very high unit cost — inefficient use of specialised labour
  • Scheduling space and activities is complex
  • Workers must bring everything to site — logistics intensive
Examples
  • 🚢 Shipbuilding — ship stays on slipway, workers come to it
  • 🏥 Open-heart surgery — patient on table, surgical team comes to patient
  • 🏗️ Bridge/skyscraper construction
  • 🍽️ Fine dining restaurant — chef/waiter comes to your table
  • 🖥️ Mainframe computer maintenance on-site
🏢

2. Functional (Process) Layout

Similar resources or processes are grouped together in departments/areas. Products/customers move between these functional areas as needed — and because each has different needs, flow patterns are varied and complex. Also called "process layout."

✅ Advantages
  • High product and mix flexibility — handles huge variety
  • Relatively robust — if one area breaks down, others continue
  • Workers develop deep expertise in their function
  • Equipment grouped for efficient maintenance
❌ Disadvantages
  • Low utilisation of equipment (not always busy)
  • High work-in-process inventory (queues between departments)
  • Complex, unpredictable flow — hard to control
  • Long throughput times
Examples
  • 🏥 Hospital — X-ray dept, labs, pharmacy, wards all separate functional areas
  • 🏪 Supermarket — dairy aisle, frozen foods, bakery, meat counter all grouped by type
  • 📚 Library — loan books, reference section, online terminals, study desks, journal stacks all in separate zones
  • 🏭 Engineering workshop — lathes together, milling machines together, drilling together
🔲

3. Cell Layout

Transformed resources are pre-selected to move to a specific cell that contains all the transforming resources needed for their type. The cell handles a family of similar products/customers. It is an attempt to bring order to the complexity of functional layout while retaining some flexibility.

✅ Advantages
  • Good compromise — fast throughput with some flexibility
  • Team work within the cell creates good staff motivation
  • Clearer, more manageable flow than functional layout
  • Lower WIP than functional layout
❌ Disadvantages
  • Can be costly to rearrange existing functional layout into cells
  • May need more total plant/equipment (some duplication across cells)
  • Cell boundaries can become rigid — cross-cell communication needed
Examples
  • 💻 Laptop factory — dedicated cell for assembling Brand A laptops, another cell for Brand B
  • 🥪 Supermarket "grab & go lunch" zone — all lunch items together for time-pressed customers
  • 🤰 Maternity unit in a general hospital — a self-contained unit for a defined patient group
  • 🏭 Focused factory within a factory — a cell dedicated to one product family
➡️

4. Product (Line/Flow) Layout

Transforming resources are located entirely for the convenience of the transformed resource. Every product/customer follows the same pre-arranged route through resources arranged in the sequence of process steps. Flow is predictable and easy to control. Also called "line layout" or "flow layout."

✅ Advantages
  • Low unit cost for high volumes — maximum efficiency
  • Easy to control and schedule
  • Short throughput time — products move quickly
  • Opportunities for specialised equipment at each stage
❌ Disadvantages
  • Low mix flexibility — hard to accommodate different products
  • Not robust to disruption — one breakdown halts everything
  • Work can be very repetitive — demotivating for staff
  • High capital investment in dedicated equipment
Examples
  • 🚗 Car assembly line — every model follows the exact same sequence of stations
  • 🍽️ Self-service cafeteria — starter → main → dessert → drinks → checkout
  • 📄 Loan application processing — every application goes: receive → assess → decision → document → pay out
  • 🧼 Bottling plant — fill → cap → label → pack → palletise (same for every bottle)

Mixed Layouts — How Real Operations Combine Types

Most real-world operations use hybrid or mixed layouts — combining elements of different basic types in different parts of the operation, or using "pure" types in separate zones.

Hospital Example

A hospital's overall layout is functional (X-ray department, labs, wards, pharmacy all grouped by function). But within that:

  • Surgical theatres: Fixed-position layout (patient on table, surgical team comes to them)
  • Blood processing laboratory: Product/line layout (samples flow through a fixed sequence of tests)
  • Maternity unit: Cell layout (self-contained unit serving a defined patient group)
  • X-ray department: Functional layout (different machines for chest X-ray, CT scan, MRI)
Restaurant Complex Example
  • Fine dining section: Fixed-position — waiter brings everything to your table
  • Buffet section: Cell layout — separate starter cell, main course cell, dessert cell
  • Self-service cafeteria: Product/line layout — move along the service line in sequence
  • Kitchen: Functional layout — grill area, prep area, pastry section, plating section all separate

Queuing Methods

The Psychology and Management of Queues

Almost nobody likes waiting. An average person may spend up to 30 minutes per day waiting in line — and research shows people overestimate idle wait time by up to 36%. Queues are not just operational problems; they are customer experience problems.

Queues occur when demand temporarily exceeds capacity. They can be:

  • Physical: People standing in a line at one location (post office)
  • Geographically dispersed: Patients waiting at home for hospital appointments
  • Virtual: Online waiting rooms; Uber showing "your driver is 3 minutes away"
  • Psychological: The perception of waiting — even when no formal queue exists
Managing Perceived Wait Time
  • Occupied time feels shorter: Give customers something to do (menus, entertainment, screens)
  • Uncertain waits feel longer: Tell customers how long the wait will be
  • Unexplained waits feel longer: Tell customers why they're waiting
  • Unfair waits feel longest: Use snake queues (one line, multiple servers) to ensure perceived fairness

Operations Strategy with Respect to Process Selection

Strategic Considerations in Process Selection

Hire Managers with Both Technical and Managerial Skills

Pure engineers make technically optimal but managerially impractical decisions ("white elephants" — over-engineered, expensive solutions that don't fit the business). Managers need both perspectives to select appropriate processes.

Build Flexibility as a Competitive Strategy

Flexibility should be designed into processes at all levels — not just as a reactive afterthought. Markets change; processes that cannot adapt quickly become liabilities.

Capital Intensity: Judicious Use of Automation

Capital intensity = the mix of equipment and labour in a process. More automation generally lowers variable cost per unit but raises fixed costs. Unnecessary automation can increase costs (especially if demand is variable) and create inventory backlogs. The decision must be driven by volume, variety, and demand variability — not by the appeal of new technology.

Amazon automates high-volume, repetitive picking tasks with robots, but retains human pickers for awkward or fragile items where robots still underperform. This is judicious automation — not automation for its own sake.

Revision Q&A

Click each question to reveal the full model answer

Define operations management and explain why it is important to a business.

Definition: OM is the activity of managing resources devoted to the production and delivery of products and services. It is the business function responsible for planning, coordinating, and controlling those resources.

Importance: OM matters because it (1) reduces costs through efficiency, (2) increases revenue by delivering quality and service that satisfies customers, (3) reduces the need for investment by maximising use of existing capacity, and (4) enhances innovation by building operational skills and knowledge.

Example: At IKEA, OM is responsible for the store layout, product replenishment, staff deployment, and quality of customer service — all of which directly drive IKEA's low-cost, high-volume strategy.

Explain the Input–Transformation–Output model with examples.

Every operation takes inputs (transformed resources: materials, information, customers; plus transforming resources: facilities, staff), subjects them to a transformation process (design, planning and control, improvement), and produces outputs (products and services) that add value for customers.

  • A car factory transforms steel and components (materials) using robots and workers (transforming resources) into finished cars
  • A hospital transforms sick patients (customers) using doctors and equipment into healthier patients
  • A bank transforms financial information using IT systems and advisors into mortgage products
What are the five performance objectives? Give an industry example for each.
  • Quality: Doing things right. DHL ensures parcels are undamaged and correctly addressed.
  • Speed: Doing things fast. McDonald's targets under 3-minute order-to-hand-over time.
  • Dependability: Doing things on time. FedEx guarantees next-day delivery by 10 AM.
  • Flexibility: Changing what you do. A job shop can switch between different machined components quickly.
  • Cost: Doing things cheaply. Ryanair strips every non-essential cost to offer the lowest fares.

Note: improving quality, speed, dependability, and flexibility also reduces cost — fewer defects, less WIP, less rescheduling all save money.

Explain the 4 Vs framework and its strategic implications.

The 4 Vs (Volume, Variety, Variation, Visibility) profile an operation and reveal its cost structure and strategic position.

  • High volume → repetition, specialisation, lower unit cost
  • High variety → customisation, flexibility needed, higher unit cost
  • High variation in demand → flexible capacity needed, hard to schedule, higher cost
  • High visibility → customer contact skills essential, cannot be fully industrialised

Strategic implication: operations managers must understand their 4 Vs position and decide whether it is appropriate for their market. Moving up volume while reducing variety cuts cost but reduces responsiveness. The choice must align with competitive strategy.

What are the five manufacturing process types? When would each be used?
  • Project: One-off, complex, large-scale products. E.g. building a bridge. Use when: every output is unique, coordination of many specialist skills required.
  • Jobbing: Very small quantities, highly customised. E.g. bespoke tailor. Use when: extremely low volumes, high variety, skilled generalist workers available.
  • Batch: Medium volumes of standard products with repeating demand. E.g. bakery. Use when: demand is predictable but variety still needed; set-ups are feasible.
  • Mass (line): High volume, low variety. E.g. car assembly line. Use when: very high stable demand for a limited product range; high capital investment justified.
  • Continuous: Extremely high volume, single product. E.g. oil refinery. Use when: demand is essentially unlimited, product is homogeneous, start/stop costs are prohibitive.
Describe and compare the four basic facility layout types.
  • Fixed-position: Product stays still; resources come to it. Best for very large or delicate products. High flexibility, very high unit cost. E.g. shipbuilding, surgery.
  • Functional: Similar resources grouped together. Different products take different routes. High variety and robustness; complex flow, high WIP, low utilisation. E.g. hospital, library.
  • Cell: Resources grouped into cells for product families. Good compromise of speed and flexibility; team motivation benefits. Some duplication of equipment. E.g. maternity unit, focused factory.
  • Product (line): Resources arranged in sequence of process steps. Predictable flow; low unit cost; easy to control; low flexibility; vulnerable to breakdowns. E.g. car assembly, cafeteria service line.

Key: as volume increases and variety decreases, the appropriate layout moves from fixed-position → functional → cell → product.

What is process mapping and how is it used to improve operations?

Process mapping describes processes using standardised symbols (operation, inspection, transport, delay, storage, decision) to show how activities relate to each other. It makes the invisible visible.

How it improves operations:

  • Identifies non-value-adding steps (delays, unnecessary transport)
  • Reveals where decision complexity slows flow
  • Allows before/after comparison when redesigning processes
  • Supports staff training — everyone understands the full process
  • Highlights potential fail points before they cause problems

Example: Prêt a Manger's process map showed that the old customised sandwich process created bottlenecks at peak hours. The new process pre-assembles bread bases, using a decision node to redirect standard orders to a fast path — reducing throughput time significantly.

What is the Triple Bottom Line and why does it matter to operations managers?

The Triple Bottom Line measures business performance across three dimensions: Economic (profit), Social (impact on communities and employees), and Environmental (impact on the natural environment).

It matters to operations managers because: (1) regulators increasingly require environmental reporting, (2) customers and employees favour ethical, sustainable organisations, (3) resource efficiency (a sustainability goal) also reduces costs, and (4) supply chain scrutiny means operations must ensure suppliers also meet standards.

Example: Unilever's Sustainable Living Plan requires operations to halve environmental footprint, improve wellbeing for 1 billion people, and double the business size — all simultaneously, requiring operations strategy to balance all three.

Explain the Balanced Scorecard and its relevance to operations strategy.

The Balanced Scorecard (Kaplan & Norton) translates vision and strategy into performance measures across four perspectives: Financial ("how do we appear to shareholders?"), Customer ("how do we appear to customers?"), Internal Processes ("which processes must we excel at?"), and Learning & Growth ("how do we sustain improvement?").

For operations managers, the BSC prevents optimising only financial metrics at the expense of long-term capability. An operation that cuts training (L&G) to save money may improve short-term profits but harm long-term process quality and customer satisfaction. The BSC forces balance across all four perspectives, ensuring operations strategy is aligned with the overall organisational vision.

What is productivity, how is it measured, and how can it be improved?

Definition: P = Outputs ÷ Inputs. The more output per unit of input, the more productive the operation.

Types of measurement:

  • Single-factor productivity: Output per unit of one input (e.g. units per worker-hour)
  • Multi-factor productivity: Output relative to several inputs combined (e.g. output per £ of labour + material cost)
  • Total factor productivity: All outputs relative to all inputs

How to improve it: Lean techniques (eliminate waste), process redesign (remove non-value-adding steps), automation, better scheduling (match staffing to demand), staff training (faster, fewer errors), and capacity management (spread fixed costs over more units).
